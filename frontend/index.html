Why does the ISL signal fail?
Two failure modes:

1. Earth occultation — Earth physically blocks the straight-line path when the satellites are on opposite sides of the globe. Detected geometrically: if the closest point on the satellite-to-satellite line is within Earth's radius + 100 km atmosphere margin, the path is blocked.

2. Insufficient link margin — The received signal is too weak. The dominant loss is free-space path loss (FSPL), which grows as:
FSPL = 20·log₁₀(4πdf/c)
e.g. at 26 GHz, 5 000 km → FSPL ≈ 195 dB.

The link closes when:
P_rx = P_tx + G_tx + G_rx − FSPL
P_rx > N_floor + SNR_req + margin

where N_floor = kTB + NF is the thermal noise floor.

Increasing antenna gain (e.g. +5 dBi each side = +10 dB) or transmit power directly extends the maximum range. Higher frequency increases FSPL but allows smaller, higher-gain dish antennas.
